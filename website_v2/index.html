The price of integration

When apartheid's sanctions fell away in 1994, South Africa re-entered the world economy. Three decades later, GDP per capita has risen by a third — and the top 10% now claim 65% of pre-tax national income. This page lets you interrogate that pairing.

1 · The question

Did post-apartheid South Africa's rapid integration into world goods, capital and norms markets buy growth at the cost of distribution?

In 1994 South Africa inherited an economy that had been deliberately closed off for three decades: sanctions, divestment and autarkic industrial policy had left trade at just 37% of GDP and the Johannesburg Stock Exchange largely cut off from foreign capital. Two macro strategies defined the transition. The Reconstruction and Development Programme (RDP, 1994) promised redistribution, housing, electrification and a developmental state. Twenty months later it was shelved in favour of Growth, Employment and Redistribution (GEAR, 1996): a self-described orthodox macro framework — fiscal consolidation, inflation targeting, trade liberalisation, capital account opening and partial privatisation.

Three decades of data are now in. This page runs hundreds of exploratory regressions across four public datasets, flags every association that clears a 5% threshold, and adjusts for multiple testing. The aim is not to declare a winner between RDP and GEAR — that debate is genuinely political — but to put honest descriptive statistics next to the clearest causal evidence from the literature.

What this page is not. A single-country time series with T ≤ 35 cannot identify causal effects. Coefficients here should be read as conditional correlations. Causal claims in the prose rely on papers in the knowledge base: synthetic-control evidence on sanctions (Evenett; Chatterjee-Czajka-Gethin), local-labour-market designs on trade liberalisation (Erten-Leight-Tregenna), and wealth-distribution accounts (Chatterjee et al., WIL 2021).

    3 · Macro evidence

    Integration, measured as (exports + imports) / GDP, more than doubled between 1990 and 2022. FDI, always modest, rode the same wave with a lag.

    Trade liberalisation was rapid: tariffs fell from an average of roughly 23% in 1993 to around 8% by 2005. In the headline OLS, a one-percentage-point increase in Trade/GDP is associated with +1.4% higher GDP per capita (HAC, p < 0.001) — but so is higher unemployment (+0.20pp, p = 0.036) and a higher top-10% income share (+0.69pp, p < 0.001). The direction of association is unambiguous; the direction of causation is not. Engle-Granger cointegration for log GDP pc and Trade/GDP fails to reject the no-cointegration null (p ≈ 0.48), so the long-run relationship should be read cautiously.

    4 · Inequality evidence

    Three complementary lenses: pre-tax income shares (WID), wealth shares (WID), and survey-based Gini (WIID / WDI).

    The central puzzle shows up in the inequality data: opening the economy and slimming the state did not compress the distribution as classical liberal theory would predict; it widened it. From 1994 onwards, the top-decile income share rose by roughly 18 percentage points. The bottom half's share shrank by a third. Wealth is an even starker story: the top 10% hold roughly 85% of household wealth.

    The Erten–Leight–Tregenna (2019) paper isolates the causal piece: districts more exposed to the tariff cuts of 1994–2005 saw lower employment, formalisation and wages — and the hit fell hardest on Black workers and women. That evidence, not the correlations above, is what justifies the "price of integration" framing.

    5 · Governance evidence

    The six Worldwide Governance Indicators pillars, normalised to 0–1 and averaged across underlying sources.

    The WGI composite peaks around 2008 and slides during the Zuma administration (2009–2018), with a partial recovery after. The regression battery picks up a positive association between the WGI composite and GDP growth (β ≈ 40.5, HAC p = 0.036, n = 26) — but this correlation does not identify the causal impact of state capacity on growth, especially given the short panel and the GFC shock embedded in it.

    Caveats, in order of severity

    1. Small n. At most 35 annual observations. Confidence intervals are wide even when p-values look small.
    2. Autocorrelation. Many specs have Durbin-Watson below 1.5. HAC SEs partially mitigate this; the "DW < 1.5 filter" above removes those specs at the reader's request.
    3. Multiple testing. We estimate hundreds of specifications. Bonferroni and Benjamini-Hochberg adjusted p-values are shown alongside raw p; a finding that survives BH is more credible.
    4. No identification. Correlations do not settle the policy debate. For causal claims the paper relies on synthetic-control (sanctions), local-labour-market designs (trade liberalisation) and distributional accounts (WIL 2021).
    5. Coverage gaps. WIID Gini drops out after 2017; WDI Gini is sparser still. Comparisons across series must account for survey design differences.
