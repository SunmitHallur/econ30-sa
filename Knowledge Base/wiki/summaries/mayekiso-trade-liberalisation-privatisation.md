## Bibliographic details

- **Authors:** Cwenga Mayekiso, Musawenkosi Lamla (Walter Sisulu University)
- **Published:** *Insights into Regional Development* 7(4), December 2025, 262–277
- **DOI:** 10.70132/j4269338243 (open access)

## Research question

Over 1994–2024, how did trade liberalisation and privatisation affect **employment**, **manufacturing output**, and **inequality** in South Africa?

## Method

- **Mixed-methods retrospective:**
  - Trend analysis of public macro data (Stats SA, World Bank, DTIC, Engineering News).
  - Semi-structured interviews with 15 MA Political Science students.
- Qualitative coding of perceptions of deindustrialisation, job insecurity, skill mismatch.

## Stylised facts the paper documents

- **Manufacturing jobs:** 1.4 million (2005) → 1.09 million (2021), a loss of ~309 000.
- **Manufacturing share of GDP:** 19.1% → 13.2%.
- **Unemployment (Q1 2024):** 32.9% (official); youth (15–34) 45.5%.
- **Gini:** roughly 0.63 → 0.67 over the period.
- **Tariff regime:** sustained reductions after 1994 under WTO, SADC, and EU-SA TDCA commitments.

## Theoretical framing

Triangulates three lenses:

1. **Premature deindustrialisation** (Rodrik 2015, 2016, 2021) — developing countries losing manufacturing share at lower income levels than historical industrialisers.
2. **Political economy of labour and distribution** — trade effects mediated by labour-market institutions, bargaining, redistribution policies.
3. **State capability / new industrial policy** — prescriptive: DTIC reviews, EV and automotive white papers (2023–24), localisation.

## Core argument

Trade liberalisation in SA, **unaccompanied by effective industrial and labour policies**, exacerbated structural vulnerability: labour-intensive manufacturing (textiles, apparel, metal products) contracted sharply; capital-intensive export-oriented sectors (chemicals, autos) stabilised but did not absorb displaced workers. The result is a labour-absorption paradox — openness without broad-based employment gains — and entrenched inequality.

Privatisation, which the ILRIG handbook and other heterodox literature treat as a parallel lever (e.g. Telkom, Transnet, Eskom restructuring), is argued to have similarly shifted risks and rents rather than delivered efficient service expansion.

## Policy recommendations

- Targeted **reindustrialisation** via localisation, procurement preferences, and sector-specific incentives.
- **Skills and labour-market policies** aligned with labour-intensive sector demand.
- **Redistributive policies** to offset the skill bias of openness.
- A **mixed economy** balancing openness and strategic state intervention.

## Strengths and limitations

- Valuable **synthesis of 30 years of stylised facts** through Stats SA / DTIC / World Bank sources, with clear documentation.
- Qualitative component is limited (15 postgraduate students, convenience sample) and should be read as illustrative rather than representative.
- Not causal: does not separate trade liberalisation from global automation, offshoring, or domestic policy failures (electricity, logistics, crime).
