## Bibliographic details

- **Author:** African National Congress (ANC), under Nelson Mandela
- **Year:** 1994 (pre-election ANC policy framework; followed by the September 1994 RDP White Paper)
- **Type:** Primary policy document, ~146 pages

## Core claim

The RDP is framed as an "integrated, coherent socio-economic policy framework" to eradicate apartheid and build a democratic, non-racial, non-sexist South Africa. It breaks with the orthodox "growth first, redistribute later" view by arguing that growth and redistribution are **parts of a single process**, linked by an infrastructural programme (housing, water, electricity, telecoms, transport, health, education) that meets basic needs and simultaneously unlocks productive capacity.

## Six basic principles

1. Integrated and sustainable programme.
2. A **people-driven** process (development as participation, not delivery).
3. Peace and security for all (non-partisan security forces; legitimate judiciary).
4. **Nation-building** — rejects a "first-world / third-world" dualism and trickle-down.
5. Link reconstruction and development (growth + redistribution as one integrated programme, with infrastructure as the key link).
6. Democratisation of the state and society (beyond periodic elections).

## Five key programmes

1. **Meeting basic needs** — jobs, land, housing (1 million+ houses in five years), water and sanitation for all, electrification of 2.5 million homes, clean-water access for 12 million without it, universal primary healthcare, social security, land redistribution.
2. **Developing human resources** — education and training, arts and culture, sport, youth development.
3. **Building the economy** — industry, trade and commerce, resource-based industries, infrastructure upgrading, financial-sector reform, labour rights, Southern African regional policy.
4. **Democratising state and society** — constituent assembly, security-sector reform, restructuring the public sector and parastatals, strengthening civil society and local government.
5. **Implementing the RDP** — coordinating structures, planning frameworks, financing.

## Diagnostic framing

- Apartheid produced one of the world's most unequal income distributions; underdevelopment of bantustans and townships contrasted with white-owned commercial farms and well-resourced suburbs.
- Conglomerate concentration; cheap-labour policies; employment segregation; under-investment in R&D and training in protected industries.
- The RDP explicitly rejects "piecemeal and uncoordinated policies" and trickle-down development.

## Economic posture

- Keynesian-developmental in tone: a **leading and enabling** role for the state in a mixed economy.
- Argues that meeting basic needs builds productive capacity and export potential.
- The growth path "must ensure more equitable distribution of incomes" (quoted later as a benchmark against [[gear-strategy|GEAR]]).
- Commits to a **living wage** as a precondition for the required level of growth.

## Significance

The RDP is the founding macro-policy vision of the post-apartheid state. Within eighteen months it was partly displaced (critics say effectively replaced) by [[gear-strategy|GEAR]] (1996), which adopted a more neo-liberal fiscal-deficit-targeting, trade-liberalising stance. The distance between the 1994 RDP and the 1996 GEAR is the pivot of much of the subsequent [[political-economy-of-transition|political-economy literature]] (see e.g. [[rdp-to-gear-transition|Adelzadeh 1996]]).
