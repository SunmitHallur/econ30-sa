## Bibliographic details

- **Publisher:** World Bank, Washington DC
- **Series:** South Africa Economic Update, Edition 13
- **Year:** 2021
- **Length:** ~55 pages (Executive Summary + Part 1 Recent Economic Developments + Part 2 Labour Markets & COVID impacts)

## Purpose

The 13th edition in the World Bank's semi-regular SA Economic Update series. Provides the Bank's diagnostic of the COVID-19 shock on the SA economy and argues the pandemic is best understood as an *amplifier* of pre-existing structural weaknesses rather than a standalone shock.

## Key stylised facts it documents

- **GDP**: Real GDP contracted **7% in 2020** — the deepest recession since the end of apartheid, exceeded among major emerging economies only by Argentina, India, Mexico.
- **Fiscal**: Fiscal deficit widened to **12.9% of GDP** in 2020/21; public debt rose to **78.8% of GDP**. Debt-service burden approaching **5% of GDP**.
- **Labour market**: ~**1.5 million jobs** lost by end-2020; wages for the still-employed fell 10–15%; average working-age South African was ~18% worse off at end-2020 vs start.
- **Unemployment**: rose by >1 million to ~7 million unemployed; **unemployment rate 33%** — a record high.
- **Inactivity**: ~17 million working-age South Africans not in the labour force; ~3 million classified as discouraged.
- **Self-employment**: only **~10% of SA jobs**, versus ~30% in peer upper-middle-income economies — identified as a key under-exploited margin.

## Three interconnected challenges (the Bank's framing)

1. **Growth** — the 2010s was a "lost decade"; per-capita GDP declined in real terms from 2015; broad-based structural reform (energy, freight rail, ports, regulation) needed to restart private investment.
2. **Jobs** — persistent high unemployment, low participation, very low self-employment; the pandemic hit poorer workers harder in employment losses, hit richer workers harder on wages.
3. **Debt** — entered the crisis already fiscally stressed; rising contingent liabilities from SOEs, especially **Eskom**; SA's deep domestic financial sector provides resilience but fiscal path is unsustainable absent reform.

## Policy proposals (Part 2)

Four categories of intervention to strengthen job recovery:

1. **Strengthen labour-market linkages of temporary government programmes**
   - Expand and deepen the **Employment Tax Incentive (ETI)** targeting youth and women.
   - Extend the **Temporary Employer/Employee Relief Scheme (TERS)** for lockdown-affected sectors until recovery consolidates.
2. **Consider a time-bound moratorium** (12–18 months) on regulations that raise the real cost of labour — explicitly linked to the pandemic and vulnerable worker protection.
3. **Revisit entrepreneurship and self-employment policy**
   - Relax legal constraints on informal / micro enterprises.
   - Scale up combined entrepreneurial-training + start-up-grant programmes.
   - Headline claim: raising SA's self-employment rate to peer averages could **halve** the unemployment rate.
4. **Improve governance of active labour-market programmes**
   - Incorporate job-search and training into EPWP and the Presidential Employment Stimulus.
   - Incentivise labour intermediation services.
   - Use UIF funds to supplement part-time wages.
   - Build monitoring and evaluation for programme targeting.

## Distributional findings

- **Employment losses** concentrated in the **bottom 40%** of the income distribution — low-skill, informal-adjacent jobs in trade, accommodation, and transport were hardest hit by lockdowns.
- **Wage losses** concentrated at the **top of the distribution** — richer workers kept their jobs but took larger pay cuts.
- Net effect: inequality rose, because the employment channel dominates the wage channel in income terms.

## Methodology and data

- Relies on **Stats SA QLFS** micro-data for labour-market analysis (see [[quarterly-labour-force-survey]]).
- Uses **NIDS-CRAM** (National Income Dynamics Study Coronavirus Rapid Mobile Survey) for wage-level detail during lockdowns.
- World Bank macro-fiscal modelling for the growth and debt projections.

## How it connects

- Provides the **World Bank's institutional framing** — complementary to the Stats SA primary QLFS releases ([[stats-sa-qlfs-p0211-2023q3]], [[stats-sa-qlfs-p0211-2025q1]]) and the retrospective in [[mayekiso-trade-liberalisation-privatisation]].
- Its policy framing (labour-market flexibility + entrepreneurship + fiscal discipline) sits within the mainstream/orthodox tradition and contrasts with the heterodox reading in [[political-economy-of-transition-handbook-part1|ILRIG (2023)]].
- Relevant for the inequality narrative in [[inequality-in-south-africa]] — documents the COVID-era widening of the income distribution.

## Caveats

- A **diagnostic report**, not a causal study; its attribution of pre-pandemic weaknesses to structural factors is narrative, not identified.
- Policy recommendations carry the Bank's usual priors (flexibility, deregulation, entrepreneurship-led growth) which are contested in SA policy debate.
- Published in 2021 — some projections (e.g. 2021 GDP growth of 4%) were superseded by later data.
