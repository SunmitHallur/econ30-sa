## Bibliographic details

- **Publisher:** Department of Finance, Republic of South Africa, June 1996
- **Ministers:** Trevor Manuel (Finance), Gill Marcus (Deputy), Maria Ramos (Acting DG)
- **Technical team:** Andre Roux, Iraj Abedian, Alan Hirsch, Stephen Gelb, Brian Kahn, Servaas van der Berg, Luiz Pereira da Silva & Richard Ketley (World Bank), etc.
- **Type:** Primary government strategy document, ~25 pages

## Headline targets (the "integrated scenario", 1996–2000)

- **GDP growth:** reach 6.1% by 2000 (avg. 4.2%).
- **New jobs:** 400 000 per year by 2000 (avg. 270 000).
- **Fiscal deficit:** reduce from 5.1% to 3.0% of GDP.
- **Average tariff:** from 10% to 6% of imports.
- **Inflation:** held below 10%.
- **Real non-gold export growth:** ~8.4% avg.
- **Real private investment growth:** ~11.7% avg.

## Core logic

> "Sustained growth on a higher plane requires a transformation towards a competitive outward-oriented economy."

The strategy is an orthodox stabilisation-plus-liberalisation package:

1. **Fiscal tightening** — faster deficit reduction to crowd in private investment and ease pressure on interest rates.
2. **Monetary policy** focused on inflation reduction, with **positive real interest rates**.
3. **Exchange-rate policy** — keep the real effective rate stable at a competitive level after the 1996 depreciation.
4. **Further relaxation of exchange controls** (gradual dismantling).
5. **Accelerated tariff reductions** beyond WTO commitments, to compensate for the depreciation and contain input prices.
6. **Tax incentives** for new, labour-absorbing investment (accelerated depreciation; tax holidays).
7. **Public-sector asset restructuring** — partial privatisation / equity partners in Telkom, transport, energy.
8. **Expansionary public infrastructure** programme to "crowd in" private investment.
9. **"Regulated flexibility"** in the labour market — differentiated minimum wages, lower rates for young trainees, greater bargaining flexibility.
10. **National social agreement** to keep wage and price increases within productivity growth.

## How it differs from the [[reconstruction-and-development-programme|RDP]]

- GEAR treats **redistribution as an outcome of growth**, not a co-equal objective.
- **Employment** is driven mainly by private investment responding to lower interest rates and tariff reductions — a crowding-out / crowding-in story.
- **Trade** policy is to "consolidate and accelerate" liberalisation, not to use tariffs for industrial policy.
- **Labour-market** posture is "flexibility" rather than protection.

## Model and projections

- Uses (primarily) the South African Reserve Bank macro model plus a Department of Finance expenditure model.
- Projects that if wage moderation, fiscal consolidation, and additional FDI materialise, the "integrated scenario" delivers 4.2% avg growth, 2.4% average current-account deficit, and 270 000 jobs/year.
- The "base scenario" (with slower reform) is projected at ~2.8% growth and ~104 000 jobs/year.

## Political economy

- Adopted without extensive consultation with labour (COSATU, SACP) or civil society; widely read as a unilateral pivot to neoliberal macro policy, hence the [[rdp-to-gear-transition|Adelzadeh (1996) critique]] and the broader [[political-economy-of-transition|political-economy of transition]] literature.
- Many of its targets (6% growth, 400k jobs/year) were **not met**. Tariff and fiscal targets largely were.

## Significance for the project

GEAR is the operational pivot of post-apartheid macroeconomic policy and the empirical anchor of most subsequent liberalisation studies (Erten-Leight-Tregenna, Driver, Edwards-Lawrence, Rodrik). It defines the period in which tariffs fall, exchange controls loosen, deficits tighten, and the manufacturing base begins its contraction — the stylised facts that the rest of the knowledge base tries to explain.
