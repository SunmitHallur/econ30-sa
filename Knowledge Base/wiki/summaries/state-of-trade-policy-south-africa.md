## Bibliographic details

- **Authors:** Rashad Cassim, Donald Onyango, Dirk Ernst van Seventer (Trade and Industrial Policy Strategies, TIPS)
- **Published:** January 2004 (TIPS Monograph, ISBN 1-919982-04-3, ~177 pages)
- **Peer reviewers noted:** Prof. Merle Holden (Univ. of Natal), Dr Carolyn Jenkins (Oxford)

## Purpose

A comprehensive stock-taking of SA trade policy a decade after democracy: tariff structure, WTO commitments, effective protection, exchange-rate behaviour, welfare analysis, employment effects, and export competitiveness. It is the most detailed public-domain diagnostic of the SA tariff schedule c. 2000–2001.

## Periodisation of trade liberalisation

- **First episode (1972–1977)** — modest.
- **Second episode (1983–1990)** — replacement of quantitative restrictions with tariffs; Industrial Development Corporation (IDC) reforms.
- **Post-1990 liberalisation** — phasing out of import surcharges; simplification of the tariff schedule; rationalisation of tariff lines.
- **Post-1994 / WTO (1995)** — accelerated five-year tariff phase-down; GEAR (1996) committed to faster reductions beyond WTO obligations; SADC, EU-SA TDCA, and later SACU agreements.

## Key facts on tariffs

- Tariff lines nearly halved relative to the 1980s.
- By 2000–2001 the schedule is split between MFN, EU (TDCA), and SADC preference regimes; average **import-weighted tariffs** fell from double digits in 1996 to single digits by 2000.
- Still a long tail of HS-8 lines with ad-valorem-equivalent duties above 40% (notably textiles, clothing, footwear, some auto components).
- The chapter computes **ad-valorem equivalents** for specific and mixed tariffs, which substantially changes the distribution of effective protection.
- Computes **nominal and effective rates of protection (ERP)** and an **anti-export bias** index by SIC v5 sector, showing higher ERPs in light manufacturing (clothing, auto, food processing) and an anti-export bias that compresses over the 1990s.

## Exchange rate

- Chapter 4 documents nominal and real effective exchange rate indices (1995 = 100) and argues:
  - Real depreciation in 1996 and 2001 substantially augmented trade protection beyond scheduled tariffs.
  - Export performance responds positively to real depreciation but with lags and volatility penalties.
  - Real-exchange-rate instability depresses the wage-productivity pass-through and investment decisions.

## Trade and employment

- Trade liberalisation is **not** identified as the dominant driver of manufacturing employment decline; decomposition suggests **technological change / skill-biased demand** explains a large share of the fall.
- A partial welfare analysis (small-country assumption, sector-specific import-demand elasticities) finds net welfare gains from the 2000-level tariff schedule cuts, but modest relative to the employment losses in specific industries (notably clothing and textiles).

## Export competitiveness

- Decomposes manufacturing exports by factor usage (capital-, labour-, natural-resource-intensive) and by market ("rising stars", "falling stars", "leading retreat", "lost opportunities") à la CEPII.
- SA's **rising stars** are concentrated in natural-resource and capital-intensive manufactures (autos, metals, chemicals); **falling stars** cluster in labour-intensive categories (textiles, apparel).
- Regional orientation shifts: rising share of intra-African (SADC) exports, falling share to traditional EU destinations in labour-intensive goods.

## Policy recommendations (chapter 7)

- Continue to simplify and lower the tariff schedule, but target remaining anti-export bias sectors.
- Complement tariff reduction with **export support** and industrial policy to offset employment losses in labour-intensive sectors.
- Strengthen analytical capacity (IDC, DTI, BTT) for trade-policy formulation.
- Anchor exchange-rate management for export-sector stability.
