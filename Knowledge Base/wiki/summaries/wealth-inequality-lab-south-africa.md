## Bibliographic details

- **Authors:** Aroop Chatterjee (Southern Centre for Inequality Studies, Wits); Léo Czajka (UC Louvain); Amory Gethin (World Inequality Lab / Paris School of Economics)
- **Working paper:** WIL 2021/16 (June 2021; revised version of WIL 2020/06)
- **Commissioned under:** UNU-WIDER SA-TIED project

## Research question

What is the distribution of **personal household wealth** in South Africa between 1993 and 2017, and how has it evolved since the end of apartheid?

## Method

- Combines **three micro-to-macro data streams**:
  1. **Personal Income Tax microdata (SARS, 2010–2017)** — IRP5 (employer-reported wages, pensions) and ITR12 (self-assessed mixed/rental/interest/dividend income) covering the top ~30% of the population.
  2. **Household surveys** — seven income surveys (1993–2015) and 54 quarterly Labour Force Surveys from 2000.
  3. **Macroeconomic balance sheets** — SARB household-sector assets and liabilities (housing, pensions, life insurance, bonds, equity, currency; mortgage and other debt).
- **Income-capitalisation approach** à la Saez & Zucman (2016) and Garbinti et al. (2018): capitalise observed income flows onto macro totals; rescale non-income-yielding assets (housing, currency) separately.
- Harmonised yearly microfile, 1993–2017, reweighted to match macro totals.

## Headline results

- The **top 10%** of wealth holders own **85–86%** of household wealth.
- The **top 1%** owns **~55%**.
- The **top 0.01%** (≈3 500 adults) owns a larger share of wealth than the **bottom 90%** (≈32 million adults) combined.
- The **average wealth of the bottom 50% is negative** — their liabilities exceed their assets.
- **No decline in wealth inequality** since the end of apartheid; if anything, the top 1% and top 0.01% shares drifted upwards.
- Wealth concentration is higher than in any other country for which comparable high-quality estimates exist (France, UK, US, Russia, China, India).

## Asset composition

- **Top 10%** own 55%+ of business assets and housing, and **over 99%** of bonds and stock.
- **Top 0.1%** wealth is dominated by **financial assets**.
- **Bottom 90%** wealth is mostly **owner-occupied housing and pension wealth**.
- Life-cycle wealth accumulation exists but is dwarfed by differences in **initial endowments** — a strong inheritance channel.

## Methodological contribution

- Shows that **income capitalisation + macro rescaling** yields substantially more realistic top-end wealth distributions than survey self-reports alone (which systematically under-report financial assets).
- Provides a blueprint for developing-country wealth-inequality estimation where wealth surveys are weak but PIT and balance-sheet data exist.
- Compares three methodologies (direct survey, rescaling, capitalisation) and shows capitalisation is most reliable at the top.

## Policy / framing implications

- Wealth inequality is **structural**, inherited from apartheid asset accumulation, and has **not been meaningfully addressed** by 1994–2017 policy — supporting the core claim of the [[political-economy-of-transition|political-economy handbook]] that the economic settlement left asset distribution largely untouched.
- Income-focused distributional statistics (e.g. Gini of ~0.63–0.67) understate the actual concentration at the very top once **financial and business assets** are properly measured.
- Complements [[mayekiso-trade-liberalisation-privatisation|Mayekiso & Lamla (2025)]] — their inequality story is a flow story; this is the stock story.

The series is part of the **World Inequality Database (WID.world)**; replication code and microdata access via the SA-TIED data lab.
