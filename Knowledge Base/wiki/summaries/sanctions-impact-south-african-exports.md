## Bibliographic details

- **Author:** Simon J. Evenett (World Trade Institute, University of Berne; CEPR)
- **Published:** *Scottish Journal of Political Economy* 49(5), November 2002, pp. 557––573

## Research question

What was the effect of the sanctions imposed by eight industrialised economies (Denmark, France, Great Britain, Italy, Japan, Norway, Sweden, and the US) on South African exports between 1985/86 and 1991/93, and have SA exports recovered after the sanctions were lifted?

## Method

- **Gravity equation** on bilateral trade flows, 1978–1999 (using World Bank / UN COMTRADE data).
- Benchmark sample: SA plus 30 other upper-/lower-middle-income, non-communist developing economies with GDP > $1bn and population > 1m.
- Sanction effect identified via **country-pair dummies** interacted with sanction periods, alongside distance, GDP, population.
- Heavy emphasis on **outlier diagnostics** (DFBETAs): robustness checks exclude trade-partner pairs with unusually large or small bilateral trade relative to GDP.

## Main findings

1. Naïve estimates suggest **EC sanctions** had the largest adverse effect on SA exports.
2. Once outliers are removed, the **US Comprehensive Anti-Apartheid Act (CAAA) of 1986** is the single largest shock, reducing bilateral imports from SA by about **one third**.
3. US sanctions' effect **persists beyond 1991** (when CAAA was lifted); SA exports to the US do not bounce back, consistent with hysteresis from sunk costs of export relationships.
4. EC sanctions show some **"bounce-back"** after removal.
5. Estimated **distance elasticity falls by 50%** when outliers are dropped — a methodological point: gravity-equation distance estimates are often inflated by outliers.

## Policy / methodological implications

- Sanctions can have **long-lived effects** on trade patterns, even after repeal — weakening the "remove sanctions → trade recovers" incentive argument.
- Gravity-equation estimates in North–South samples are **sensitive to sample construction**; one must test robustness to outliers.
- Consistent with [[sanctions-synthetic-control-south-africa|Uhuru (2020)]]'s finding that the macro effect of sanctions on GDP/cap persists well past 1994.

## Significance

Provides the bilateral, sector-agnostic trade-flow evidence that complements the macro-level SCM study: trade partners that imposed CAAA-style sanctions persistently imported less from SA, and this persistence is a plausible micro-foundation for the lag effect observed in GDP per capita.
