## Bibliographic details

- **Author:** Asghar Adelzadeh (National Institute for Economic Policy, commissioned by COSATU)
- **Published:** *Transformation* 31 (1996), pp. 66–95
- **Type:** Peer-reviewed critique / commentary

## Thesis

The 1996 [[gear-strategy|GEAR document]] represents a **wholesale abandonment** of the original Keynesian-developmental thrust of the [[reconstruction-and-development-programme|RDP]] in favour of an IMF-style neo-liberal stabilisation package. The paper argues GEAR is "analytically flawed, empirically unsupportable, historically unsuitable" and will fail to deliver the RDP's objectives on growth, employment, and redistribution.

## Key lines of critique

1. **Modelling problems.** GEAR's "four-model" claim is unsupported: reported results come from the SARB model only, which is opaque, was used during apartheid without restructuring, and cannot produce sectoral employment, income distribution, or the effects of government-expenditure restructuring.
2. **Growth arithmetic.** Because the current-account deficit is projected to widen and public consumption is pegged, **93% of the fiscal stimulus** to reach 4.2% growth must come from private investment — which GEAR assumes will respond to lower interest rates via a "crowding-out" story with **no empirical evidence** for South Africa.
3. **Interest-rate elasticity.** To deliver the projected jump in private investment from a deficit cut of only 0.3 pp of GDP, the implied interest-elasticity of investment is ~0.55 — implausibly high, especially given mining's share.
4. **Fiscal consolidation.** The 3% deficit target is exogenous, not model-derived; SA's debt/GDP is moderate (56% vs 72% average OECD, 95% Japan) and external debt is low — no debt-trap case.
5. **Tax stance.** Ratio and econometric studies suggest SA was under-taxed by ~3% of GDP in 1994; the regressive VAT-heavy structure and absence of capital gains tax mean progressive reform could raise revenue and equity simultaneously.
6. **Monetary policy.** Hard-wiring inflation targeting into the growth framework is contractionary: SA never had an inflation surge, but the policy forces the Reserve Bank to tighten as soon as growth picks up.
7. **Exchange controls.** No rationale is provided for removing controls on domestic residents; the post-Finrand experience showed volatile short-term flows, not the promised FDI surge.
8. **Trade and industry.** Accelerated tariff cuts to "compensate" for the depreciation squander the opportunity to use the depreciation for import substitution; there is no real industrial policy, only "industrial support measures".
9. **Export targets.** An 8.4% average non-gold export growth rate implying a jump in the export/GDP ratio from 20.9% to 25.6% in four years has **no historical precedent** among major developed or developing economies.
10. **Labour market.** GEAR assumes inflexibility; the ILO country review (Standing, Sender, Weeks 1996) and NALEDI/LRS evidence show SA's labour market is already highly flexible in employment and wages. "Regulated flexibility" risks entrenching segmentation and widening the wage gap.
11. **Income distribution.** On GEAR's own numbers, real-wage growth (≈1%) lags GDP growth (4.2%) → wage share falls → inequality will **worsen**, contradicting the RDP's stated growth-path principle.
12. **No link from equity to growth.** Adelzadeh cites the 1996 UN Human Development Report: East Asian economies combined rapid growth with *low* inequality, undermining GEAR's implicit trickle-down premise.

## What the paper recommends

- Use the **dynamic government budget constraint** to assess sustainability, not an exogenous 3% rule.
- Use public productive expenditure to **crowd in** private investment.
- Integrate monetary policy with industrial strategy (differential interest rates).
- Reject any two-tier labour market; adopt a basic floor of rights.
- Make any social accord **conditional on a growth and development framework**, not on wage restraint alone.

## Significance

This is the canonical contemporary critique of GEAR from the labour/heterodox camp and is frequently cited alongside the [[political-economy-of-transition|ILRIG handbook]] and later retrospective assessments (e.g. [[trade-liberalization-sa-manufacturing|Driver 2019]], [[mayekiso-trade-liberalisation-privatisation|Mayekiso & Lamla 2025]]) for the proposition that post-apartheid SA adopted neo-liberalism prematurely and with unforced errors.
