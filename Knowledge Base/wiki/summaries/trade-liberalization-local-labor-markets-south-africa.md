## Bibliographic details

- **Authors:** Bilge Erten (Northeastern), Jessica Leight (American University), Fiona Tregenna (University of Johannesburg, DST/NRF Chair in Industrial Development)
- **Working paper:** 25 April 2018 (later published in *Economic Development and Cultural Change*)
- **Funding:** NRF South Africa; Tregenna's Chair

## Research question

What was the **causal** impact of post-1994 tariff reductions on local labour market outcomes in South Africa — on employment, wages, formality, labour-force participation, migration, and take-up of government transfers?

## Method (Bartik / local-labour-markets)

- Build a **district-level tariff-exposure shock**: weighted average of industry tariffs, weighted by **baseline (1994) industry employment shares**.
- Exploits two sources of variation: (a) cross-industry variation in tariff cuts (textiles/clothing cut heavily; coal mining cut little), and (b) cross-district variation in industrial composition of employment.
- Individual-level labour-force survey data, 1994–2004.
- Same empirical strategy as Topalova (2007, 2010) for India; McCaig (2011) for Vietnam; Kovak (2013) for Brazil; Autor, Dorn & Hanson (2013) for the US "China shock".

## Headline results

1. Districts with larger tariff cuts experienced **significant declines in both formal and informal employment** relative to less exposed districts, driven primarily by **manufacturing** job losses.
2. **No wage effects** for those who remain employed — wages are too rigid (unions, sectoral bargaining councils) to absorb the shock.
3. Displaced workers **do not transition** into other tradable sectors or services, and **do not migrate** to less-affected districts.
4. Instead, they become **discouraged workers** or exit the labour force, raising **broad unemployment** and **non-participation**.
5. They become **more likely to receive government transfers** (disability grants, child-support grants, dependent-care grants) — echoing the Autor-Dorn-Hanson China-shock finding for the US.
6. Heterogeneity: **less-educated black and coloured workers** face a higher probability of job loss among low-education groups; no systematic gender or age heterogeneity.
7. Robust to dropping districts with very high textile or coal employment shares, and to pre-trend checks.

## Why the SA adjustment looks different from India/Brazil

The SA labour market has four unusual features:

- Persistently very high baseline unemployment (28–35%).
- Small informal sector with high barriers to entry (legacy of apartheid restrictions on black entrepreneurship, high compliance costs).
- Wage rigidity via sectoral bargaining councils.
- Under-developed manufacturing base even at the start of liberalisation.

These features mean tariff-induced shocks cannot be absorbed through wage cuts, informalisation, or migration, so they translate into non-participation and safety-net dependence — a feature of the SA labour market that differs sharply from other middle-income liberalisers.

## Significance

First **causal** evidence of the local labour-market effects of SA's 1994–2004 tariff liberalisation. It complements:

- [[trade-liberalization-sa-manufacturing|Driver (2019)]] — mark-up / productivity / exchange-rate channel.
- [[state-of-trade-policy-south-africa|Cassim et al. (2004)]] — tariff structure itself.
- [[mayekiso-trade-liberalisation-privatisation|Mayekiso & Lamla (2025)]] — 1994–2024 deindustrialisation narrative.
- [[wealth-inequality-in-south-africa|Chatterjee, Czajka & Gethin (2021)]] — the stock side of rising inequality.

It is the single strongest causal piece of evidence in the knowledge base for the claim that **tariff liberalisation contributed to job losses** in SA's tradable sector, with the losses absorbed by non-participation and transfer-grant dependence rather than by informal or service-sector absorption.
