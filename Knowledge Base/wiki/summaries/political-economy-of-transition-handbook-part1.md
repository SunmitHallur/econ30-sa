## Bibliographic details

- **Publisher:** International Labour, Research & Information Group (ILRIG), January 2023
- **Author:** Dale T. McKinley
- **Cover design:** Design for Development; printed by Hansa Print
- **Type:** Political education handbook (~72 pages), Part 1 of a two-part "Handbook of Struggle" series

## Purpose

An activist-oriented political-economy history of SA from the late 1970s through the first twenty-plus years of post-apartheid rule. Intended for popular education — for worker and community movements — but closely sourced and empirically grounded.

## Narrative arc

1. **Late 1970s / early 1980s:** Apartheid-capitalism in crisis. Gathering strikes, the rise of the UDF, COSATU's formation (1985), capital flight. The Minerals-Energy Complex (MEC) is the structural frame used throughout.
2. **Mid–late 1980s:** State of emergency (1985 onward), sanctions, divestment, collapse of the rand, apartheid's economic exhaustion. White capital begins to seek negotiated exit.
3. **1990–1992:** Unbanning of the ANC, release of Mandela, talks about talks. The policy agenda — in parallel with the negotiated transition — shifts from the "Harare-era" socialist rhetoric towards a more market-compatible programme.
4. **1992–1994:** Negotiated political settlement. "Sunset clauses" protect apartheid-era civil servants, judges, and property. The **economic settlement** is left largely intact.
5. **1994:** First democratic election; RDP adopted as the founding policy framework (see [[rdp-1994|RDP summary]]).
6. **Mid-1990s:** RDP Office closed (1996); **GEAR** adopted (1996) without negotiation with labour or civil society — the handbook treats this as the pivotal moment of the "neoliberal capture" of the ANC (see [[rdp-to-gear-transition|Adelzadeh 1996]] for the contemporary economists' critique and [[gear-1996-macroeconomic-strategy|GEAR summary]]).
7. **Late 1990s / late 2000s:** Privatisation, financialisation, rising inequality, Marikana, state capture. The handbook reads these as downstream effects of the 1994–96 economic settlement.

## The "house" metaphor

SA is described as a house whose **political landlords** changed (NP → ANC) but whose **economic landlords** (white capital, increasingly financialised) were allowed to keep the penthouse. The handbook's key claim: "liberation turns out to be little more than a political and racially framed shifting of the capitalist veranda chairs without a corresponding transformation of socio-economic foundations."

## Key analytical concepts (glossary)

- **Minerals-Energy Complex (MEC):** mining + heavy industry + energy + finance as a distinctive accumulation regime; used to argue that SA's economic path was shaped by the sectoral power of mining-finance-electricity long before and after 1994.
- **Neo-liberalism:** the ideology that displaced the RDP's Keynesian-developmental frame; associated in this narrative with GEAR, exchange-control liberalisation, asset restructuring, and privatisation.
- **Corporatisation / privatisation / commodification:** the operational levers through which state assets and services (Telkom, water, electricity, transport) were reshaped post-1994.
- **Financialisation:** capital allocation shifting from productive investment to financial speculation and debt-driven consumption.

## Strengths and limits

- Rich narrative of **class formation**, **labour mobilisation**, and **institutional politics** that macro-econometric studies rarely address.
- Heterodox, anti-capitalist frame — strong ideological perspective, openly signalled; reads best in dialogue with mainstream empirical work.
- Light on quantitative detail; best paired with [[wealth-inequality-in-south-africa|Chatterjee-Czajka-Gethin (2021)]] for the stock-inequality numbers and the [[rdp-to-gear-transition|Adelzadeh 1996]] critique for the macro-policy arithmetic.

## How it connects

This is the knowledge base's **political-economy backbone**. Treat it as the narrative skeleton onto which the quantitative studies (tariffs, sanctions, inequality, labour markets) hang.
