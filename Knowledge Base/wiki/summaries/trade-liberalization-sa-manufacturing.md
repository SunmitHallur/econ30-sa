## Research question

Revisit the debate in *Economics of Transition* 2008 (Aghion & Fedderke eds.) on whether trade liberalisation boosted or harmed SA manufacturing, using updated Bureau of Economic Research (BER) survey data through to ~2017.

## The three camps it tests

- **Aghion, Braun & Fedderke (2008):** SA manufacturing had high mark-ups that retarded productivity growth; more competition (via trade or other channels) raises productivity.
- **Edwards & Lawrence (2008):** Trade liberalisation boosted "non-commodity manufacturing" exports by lowering input costs and reducing the relative profitability of domestic sales.
- **Rodrik (2008):** Competitive pressure cut mark-ups, profitability, and manufacturing employment — a bad outcome given the "binding political constraint" of unemployment for black South Africans.

## Hypotheses re-examined

- **A1:** No trend reduction in manufacturing mark-ups post-transition.
- **A2:** More competition → higher productivity (up to an inflection point).
- **EL1:** Trade liberalisation reduced import costs for manufacturing.
- **EL2:** Trade liberalisation reduced the relative profitability of domestic sales and raised non-commodity manufacturing exports.
- **R1:** The manufacturing mark-up falls in the post-transition period.
- **R2:** Manufacturing employment falls as the mark-up falls.

## Main findings

1. A rising **real exchange rate** (real appreciation) lowers unit raw material costs (EL1-style), as expected, but also **compresses the mark-up** — exporter profitability does not improve because firms keep foreign-currency prices from rising.
2. Real appreciation **lowers export volumes** — supporting Rodrik's channel from exchange rate to manufacturing pricing power.
3. The evidence is **more consistent with Rodrik** than with Aghion et al.: competition and real-exchange-rate pressure do not translate into robust productivity or employment gains; they compress profitability and shrink the sector.
4. Manufacturing employment falls across the period, alongside a rise in the import-penetration ratio from 17% (1990) to ~42% (2012), higher in labour-intensive sectors.
5. Capital investment responds to demand and profitability, not to tariff policy directly — consistent with a "demand-constrained" view of manufacturing investment.

## Policy implication

Driver concludes that "option (iii)" — protecting or subsidising labour-intensive manufacturing activity, possibly via tax-wedge adjustments and selective protection — is more defensible than "option (iv)" — further liberalisation to squeeze mark-ups. The paper is sympathetic to a **new-industrial-policy** / [[premature-deindustrialisation|premature-deindustrialisation]] view.

## Data

- BER Manufacturing Survey micro-panel (ISIC 3) for mark-ups, input costs, export volumes, capacity utilisation.
- Real effective exchange rate, tariffs, import penetration ratios from public macro sources.
- Period: early 1990s to ~2017.

## Significance

Driver is the most direct econometric test, using SA-specific firm-level data, of whether liberalisation actually did the good things GEAR assumed. The answer is: not much; the compression of mark-ups and employment is real, but productivity and export gains are modest and concentrated. A natural complement to [[trade-liberalization-local-labor-markets-south-africa|Erten-Leight-Tregenna (2018)]] (labour side) and [[state-of-trade-policy-south-africa|Cassim et al. (2004)]] (tariff-structure side).
