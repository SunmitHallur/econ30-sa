## Definition

The **Minerals-Energy Complex (MEC)** is a concept developed by Ben Fine and Zavareh Rustomjee (1996, *The Political Economy of South Africa: From Minerals-Energy Complex to Industrialisation*). It refers to a specific South African accumulation regime built around the interdependence of mining (gold, platinum, diamonds, coal, iron ore), heavy industry (steel, chemicals, cement, fabricated metals, aluminium, base-metal refining), energy (coal-fired electricity, synthetic fuels via Sasol), and financial conglomerates (Anglo-American, Old Mutual, Rembrandt, SANLAM historically).

## Why it matters

The MEC framework argues that:

1. SA's industrial structure is **not diversified manufacturing** but a cluster around minerals processing and energy.
2. Capital accumulation is highly **concentrated** in a small number of cross-owned conglomerates, creating a distinctive form of financialised-industrial capitalism.
3. The pricing of key inputs (electricity, steel, chemicals) reflects MEC corporate governance rather than competitive markets.
4. Post-1994 industrial policy has been reshaped by, and constrained by, the MEC's political economy.

## Empirical markers

- Share of mining-plus-heavy-industry in manufacturing value added: historically ~40–50%, still > 35% in the 2010s.
- Electricity intensity of production: among the highest in the world (legacy of cheap coal from Eskom's MEC-era expansion).
- Capital/labour ratios in flagship sectors (autos, chemicals, metals) are globally high, resulting in weak employment multipliers from fixed-investment booms.
- Corporate ownership: persistent cross-holdings and pyramidal structures into the 2000s (broken up by unbundling of Anglo-American, Old Mutual, and Rembrandt in 1998–2007, but residual concentration remains).

## How the knowledge base uses the MEC concept

- [[political-economy-of-transition-handbook-part1|ILRIG handbook]] uses the MEC as its structural frame for understanding the constraints on RDP-style redistribution: the macroeconomy depends on mining-and-energy profits, and those profits depend on global commodity prices and cheap labour.
- The concept is implicit in [[premature-deindustrialisation]]: the absence of a broad, labour-intensive manufacturing base reflects the MEC's capital-intensive bias.
- Industrial-policy debates (electricity pricing, Eskom governance, coal-to-liquids, carbon tax, green industrial strategy) are all MEC questions.

## Contrasts with alternative framings

- **Orthodox growth-accounting** treats SA's industrial structure as the product of factor endowments plus apartheid-era distortions; MEC theorists argue this misses the corporate-governance channel.
- **Dutch disease / resource-curse** frameworks overlap with the MEC story but emphasise exchange-rate effects rather than corporate structure.
