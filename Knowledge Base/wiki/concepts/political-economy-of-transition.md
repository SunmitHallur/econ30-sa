## Core question

Why did the post-1994 economic settlement — despite an overwhelming political mandate for redistribution — produce only modest changes in asset distribution, income distribution, and ownership patterns?

## Two analytical traditions

**Mainstream (political-science / institutional):**

- Negotiated settlement traded sovereignty (political) for stability (economic).
- "Sunset clauses" protected apartheid-era civil servants, property rights, and pensions.
- External credit markets, IMF surveillance, and investor confidence constrained fiscal and distributive choices.
- The ANC chose credibility-by-orthodoxy to secure capital inflows.

**Heterodox / class-analytical** ([[political-economy-of-transition-handbook-part1|ILRIG]], SAMWU, political activist literature):

- Apartheid's ruling bloc (white capital, NP elites) and the ANC's negotiated bloc struck a **class compromise** that left ownership structures intact.
- The [[minerals-energy-complex]] continued to dominate capital allocation.
- Neoliberal ideology ([[neoliberalism-in-south-africa]]) captured ANC economic thinking via a small circle of advisors (GEAR drafting team), displacing the RDP's Keynesian-developmental logic.
- "Liberation without transformation" — political landlords change, economic landlords retain the penthouse.

## Key moments

1. **1990–1992**: Mt Fleur and MERG scenarios; ANC policy positions begin to shift.
2. **1993–1994**: Transitional Executive Council; first Reserve Bank independence clauses enter drafts.
3. **1996**: **GEAR announced by fiat** — no negotiation with COSATU, SACP, or the RDP constituency. Treated as the pivotal moment by both the ILRIG handbook and [[rdp-to-gear-transition|Adelzadeh (1996)]].
4. **1996–1999**: RDP Office closed; privatisation framework adopted (Telkom IPO 1997/2003).
5. **2007**: Polokwane conference — Zuma's succession as reaction to the Mbeki-GEAR era.
6. **2012**: Marikana — 34 striking Lonmin miners shot dead by SAPS; interpreted by the handbook as the denouement of the post-1994 economic settlement.
7. **2015–2018**: State capture under Zuma; governance scores decline.
8. **2024**: GNU with the DA — further rightward tilt on fiscal and industrial policy.

## The handbook's (McKinley 2023) thesis

The post-apartheid state is structured by the following institutional features:

- Independent central bank with price-stability mandate.
- Constitutionally protected property rights (§25, with "just and equitable" compensation).
- Labour-market rules that formalise bargaining councils.
- Ownership structure in mining, finance, and industry dominated by a small number of conglomerates (Anglo-American, Old Mutual, Remgro, Sasol), increasingly financialised.

Taken together, these structures produced a **growth path consistent with rising inequality** — which is what the [[wealth-inequality-lab-south-africa|WIL 2021 data]] show.

## Heterogeneous views on remediation

- **Market-compatible redistribution** (Ramaphosa's reforms; National Democratic Revolution variants): broaden ownership, improve skills, attract FDI.
- **New industrial policy** (Tregenna, Rodrik-adjacent): protect and subsidise labour-intensive manufacturing, reindustrialise via procurement and localisation.
- **Radical economic transformation / structural reconstitution** (EFF, elements of ANC left): land reform without compensation, state-led industrialisation, mining nationalisation.

The knowledge base does not resolve these; it provides the empirical ground on which they are contested.
