## Definition

A series of economic, financial, sporting, cultural, and arms sanctions imposed on the apartheid regime by the UN, individual states, and non-governmental actors between 1962 and 1994. The economically binding period is usually taken to be **1985–1994**, coinciding with the US Comprehensive Anti-Apartheid Act (CAAA, 1986), the EC package (1985–86), and the financial crisis triggered by Chase Manhattan's 1985 refusal to roll over SA debt.

## Main instruments

- **Arms embargo**: UN Security Council Resolution 418 (1977), mandatory.
- **UN General Assembly sanctions** (non-binding) — 1962 onwards.
- **US CAAA (1986)**: banned new investment, imports of key products (uranium, coal, steel, agricultural products, textiles, iron), and financial transactions.
- **EC measures (1985–86)**: restrictions on new investment, coal/iron/steel imports, krugerrand imports, cooperation with police / military.
- **Commonwealth measures (1985–86)**: partial, but included sports and cultural isolation.
- **Financial sanctions**: refusal by major banks to roll over SA debt (August 1985), leading to the debt standstill and the emergency gold sales of the late 1980s.
- **Divestment campaigns**: university endowments, pension funds, municipalities — particularly strong in the US and UK.

## Economic effects — what the knowledge base says

1. [[sanctions-synthetic-control-south-africa|Uhuru (2020)]] — using a synthetic control, estimates SA GDP per capita was about **30% below counterfactual** by 1998 — four years after sanctions were lifted. Effects are **largest after sanctions end**, suggesting strong hysteresis.
2. [[sanctions-impact-south-african-exports|Evenett (2002)]] — using a gravity equation, finds US CAAA had the single largest adverse effect, reducing bilateral SA exports to the US by about **one third**; the effect **persists beyond 1991** (when CAAA was repealed). EC sanctions show a partial bounce-back.

## Mechanisms

- **Reduction in trade volumes**, particularly in labour-intensive manufactures and primary exports.
- **Higher cost of capital**: SA faced elevated risk premia and lost access to long-term financing.
- **Divestment**: gross fixed capital formation fell sharply in the late 1980s.
- **Technology lag**: restrictions on imports of certain machinery and scientific equipment.
- **Reputational hysteresis**: once trade / investment relationships are broken, sunk costs of re-establishment take years to recover — which explains the post-1994 lag.

## The political-economy dimension

The [[political-economy-of-transition-handbook-part1|ILRIG handbook]] argues sanctions were one of the pressures that forced white capital to the negotiating table; they combined with mass mobilisation, labour action, and fiscal crisis to make apartheid economically untenable. This reading is consistent with Crawford & Klotz (1999) and Levy (1999), though empirically less testable.

## Why this matters for the project

Sanctions are the **pre-1994 inheritance** in the economic story: they shaped the initial conditions under which the transition began — low growth, depleted capital stock, weak export base, eroded manufacturing. The post-1994 macro strategy (whether RDP or GEAR) had to contend with this inheritance.
