## Headline features

- **Population 15–64**: ~41.7 million (Q1 2025).
- **Labour force**: ~25.0 million; **employed** ~16.8 million; **unemployed** ~8.2 million.
- **Strict unemployment rate**: ~32.9% (Q1 2025).
- **Expanded unemployment rate** (incl. discouraged job-seekers): ~42–45%.
- **Labour-force participation rate**: ~60%.
- **Absorption ratio** (employed / pop 15–64): ~40%.
- **Youth (15–34) unemployment**: ~45%.

Source: [[stats-sa-qlfs-p0211-2025q1]].

## Four structural features that shape the adjustment

1. **Very high baseline unemployment** (persistently 28–35% since the 1990s) — no OECD or peer middle-income economy has anything comparable.
2. **Small informal sector** with high entry barriers — the legacy of apartheid restrictions on Black entrepreneurship, compounded by relatively high compliance costs for informal firms.
3. **Wage rigidity** via sectoral bargaining councils (Labour Relations Act 1995; BCEA 1997) — wages do not respond much to negative demand shocks.
4. **Low internal migration** — households do not reliably move across provinces/districts to arbitrage local labour-market differentials.

Together, these features mean negative labour-demand shocks (e.g. tariff cuts, commodity busts, electricity load-shedding) do not get absorbed through wage cuts, informalisation, or migration. They translate into **non-participation** and **safety-net dependence** ([[trade-liberalization-local-labor-markets-south-africa|Erten-Leight-Tregenna 2018]]).

## Industrial composition

- Finance, insurance, real estate & business services is the **largest formal-sector industry** by employment.
- Manufacturing has been in secular decline: ~1.6–1.7 million jobs in 2005 → ~1.4 million today, consistent with [[premature-deindustrialisation]].
- Government and community/social services have expanded.
- Mining employment has trended down as deep-level gold mines close.

## Unemployment by demographic

- Black Africans face 4–6× the unemployment rate of Whites.
- Women face ~5 pp higher strict unemployment than men.
- Youth (15–34): about 45% strict; expanded around 60%.
- Less-educated workers bore a disproportionate share of the liberalisation-era job losses.

## Wage and productivity dynamics

- Real wages in unionised sectors rose modestly over 1994–2023.
- Labour productivity gains concentrated in capital-intensive sectors (autos, chemicals, finance).
- The **wage share** of GDP drifted down over the 2000s, rising again post-pandemic, but remains below the 1995 benchmark.

## Policy levers (de facto)

- **Social grants** (OAP, CSG, disability, SRD) — cushion but do not replace income.
- **Employment Tax Incentive** (ETI) for youth hiring, 2014 onwards — modest measured impact.
- **Presidential Employment Stimulus** — pandemic-era public works expansion, primarily education-assistants.
- **Minimum wage** (2019) — National Minimum Wage set at ~R20/hour; effects contested.
- **Sector-specific programmes**: automotive (APDP), BPO (call centres), clothing (CSP).
