## Stylised facts

- **Income Gini**: ~0.63–0.68 depending on source and concept (survey, tax, equivalised vs per capita). SA is consistently the most unequal country in global comparisons with comparable microdata.
- **Wealth concentration** ([[wealth-inequality-lab-south-africa|Chatterjee-Czajka-Gethin 2021]]):
  - Top 10%: ~85% of household wealth.
  - Top 1%: ~55%.
  - Top 0.01% (~3 500 adults) hold more wealth than the bottom 90% (~32 million).
  - Bottom 50% has **negative average net wealth**.
- **Income shares** ([[dataset-wid-south-africa|WID]]):
  - Top 10% share of pre-tax national income: ~65–66% in recent years.
  - Bottom 50% share: ~5–6%.
- **Inequality has not narrowed since 1994** — on most measures it has mildly widened.

## Sources of SA's exceptionally high inequality

1. **Apartheid's asset legacy**: exclusion of the Black majority from land, housing, formal credit, and education produced a persistent stock imbalance.
2. **Dual labour market**: extreme wage dispersion between formal-sector, unionised employment and informal or subsistence work, combined with 30%+ unemployment (see [[labour-market-south-africa]]).
3. **Returns to capital**: rising capital/labour ratio in tradable sectors, combined with high returns on financial and business assets held by the top decile.
4. **Limited redistribution**: social grants reduce income inequality somewhat (Gini moves from ~0.77 on market income to ~0.63 on disposable income) but cannot undo the wealth distribution.
5. **Education premium**: large and rising returns to tertiary schooling, combined with weak secondary-school quality for the majority.

## Measurement — three approaches

- **Household surveys** (IES, LCS, NIDS) — good for the bottom 90%, weak at the top.
- **Tax microdata** (SARS PIT) — good at the top 10%, poor coverage below tax thresholds.
- **National accounts** — totals, but no distribution.

The WIL (2021) contribution is the harmonisation of all three into a single DINA framework; see [[wealth-distribution-methodology]].

## Relation to the project's policy questions

- Trade liberalisation and manufacturing decline plausibly **worsened** labour-market inequality via local-labour-market shocks ([[trade-liberalization-local-labor-markets-south-africa|Erten-Leight-Tregenna 2018]]).
- Privatisation and financialisation plausibly **worsened** wealth inequality by concentrating asset ownership ([[mayekiso-trade-liberalisation-privatisation|Mayekiso & Lamla 2025]]).
- GEAR-era macro stabilisation reduced inflation (which mechanically favours bottom-decile consumption) but simultaneously shrank manufacturing employment.
- Social-grant expansion after 2000 reduced bottom-decile poverty but did not alter the wealth distribution.

## Cross-country benchmarking

- WIID ([[dataset-wiid-2025]]) is the right source for SA-vs-peers comparisons.
- WID ([[dataset-wid-south-africa]]) is the right source for top-share estimates.
- WDI ([[dataset-sa-wdi-panel]]) provides the headline Gini series used in many public discussions.
