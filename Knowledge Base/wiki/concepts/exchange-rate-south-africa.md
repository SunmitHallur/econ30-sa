## Regime

- **Floating** since March 1995 (exchange-control relaxation for non-residents).
- Managed only through limited foreign-currency reserve operations; no explicit target.
- **Inflation-targeting** framework (SARB 2000, 3–6% target band) implicitly disciplines the REER via interest-rate policy.

## Empirical features

- **High volatility**: annualised volatility of ZAR/USD is among the highest in major EM currencies (10–15%).
- **Carry-trade sensitivity**: the rand is a favoured carry target; global risk-off episodes drive disproportionate depreciation.
- **Commodity-price correlation**: the rand moves with the price of gold, platinum, and iron ore; **real-rand** cycles track commodity terms-of-trade.
- **Episodic crises**: 1998 (Asian/Russian), 2001 (local crisis, nearly 40% depreciation), 2008–9 (GFC), 2015 (Nenegate), 2020 (COVID), 2023 (fiscal concerns).

## Relevance for trade and manufacturing

- [[trade-liberalization-sa-manufacturing|Driver (2019)]] shows that real-rand appreciation **compresses manufacturing mark-ups** (via output prices, not input costs) and **lowers export volumes**. In other words, a stronger rand hurts tradable sectors twice — on price and on quantity.
- [[state-of-trade-policy-south-africa|Cassim et al. (2004)]] argue that real depreciation episodes (1996, 2001) were **de facto protection** that offset scheduled tariff cuts.
- The anti-export bias of the tariff schedule interacts with the exchange-rate cycle; the combined effect on exporter profitability varies through time.

## Real effective exchange rate (REER)

- **Base index**: SARB publishes REER (weighted by trade partners, CPI-deflated) in its Quarterly Bulletin.
- **Long-run level**: real appreciation 1994–2011 of ~25% relative to 1995 base; significant depreciation 2012–2020; partial recovery post-COVID.
- **Equilibrium estimates** (Frankel 2007; Cashin-Céspedes-Sahay 2004): the rand has been near or above equilibrium for most of the 2000s, implying an anti-export-sector bias.

## Why it matters for the project

- The exchange rate is a **parallel lever** to tariff policy — both shape effective protection.
- Exchange-rate volatility adds risk that raises the hurdle rate for exporter investment.
- Monetary policy's implicit impact on the REER is a point of tension between SARB's inflation mandate and the manufacturing sector's competitiveness concerns.

## Data sources

- **SARB Quarterly Bulletin** — nominal and real effective exchange rate series.
- **IMF IFS** — comparable REER for cross-country benchmarking.
- **BIS** — broad and narrow REER indices (daily).
