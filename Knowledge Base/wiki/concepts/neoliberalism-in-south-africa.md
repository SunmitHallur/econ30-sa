## Usage

"Neoliberalism" in SA usage denotes a package of policy choices associated with the post-1996 turn: fiscal austerity, tight monetary policy, tariff liberalisation, exchange-control relaxation, financial deregulation, labour-market flexibility, corporatisation and partial privatisation of state-owned enterprises, and inflation targeting. The term is **contested**: mainstream commentators treat it as a caricature of orthodox macro management; heterodox commentators treat it as a definable ideological project.

## Two canonical treatments in the knowledge base

- [[rdp-to-gear-transition|Adelzadeh (1996)]]: the **policy critique** — GEAR imports IMF/World Bank assumptions, relies on weak econometric links, and abandons the RDP's Keynesian-developmental frame.
- [[political-economy-of-transition-handbook-part1|McKinley (2023, ILRIG)]]: the **political-economy reading** — the transition from RDP to GEAR is the emblematic moment of neoliberal capture of ANC economic policy.

## Operational markers (how to identify the "neoliberal turn")

1. **Fiscal**: deficit reduction as a headline target; primary-balance rules.
2. **Monetary**: formal inflation targeting (SARB 2000); central-bank independence.
3. **Trade**: unilateral tariff liberalisation beyond WTO commitments; FTAs with EU and SADC.
4. **Capital account**: resident exchange-control relaxation, 1995–2003; institutional investor prudential limits loosened.
5. **SOE restructuring**: corporatisation (Transnet, Eskom), partial privatisation (Telkom 1997/2003), debate over Iscor, SAA, Denel.
6. **Labour market**: wage restraint, calls for two-tier wage structures, youth employment subsidies framed as offsetting "labour-market rigidities".
7. **Financialisation**: deepening of JSE listings, SA banks' international expansion, growth of household debt.

## The counter-narrative

- SA remained **relatively statist** on fiscal spending: total government expenditure ~30% of GDP throughout, higher than most peers.
- SA **did not fully privatise** — Eskom, Transnet, SAA remained state-owned through the 2010s.
- SA **retained strong labour-market institutions**: bargaining councils, LRA, BCEA.
- Inflation targeting is a globally standard monetary regime, not distinctively "neoliberal".
- Hence some mainstream economists reject the label: GEAR was pragmatic orthodox macro, not doctrinal neoliberalism.

## Why the disagreement matters

Different framings imply different counterfactuals:

- If GEAR was **just good macro**, its disappointing distributive outcomes reflect exogenous shocks, structural legacies, or implementation failures — not the policy framework itself.
- If GEAR was **an ideological project**, the same outcomes reflect the policy framework's priors — and alternative framings (RDP, MERG, new industrial policy) would have delivered better outcomes.

The knowledge base does not resolve this; readers need to understand both framings to interpret the empirical evidence in subsequent summaries.
