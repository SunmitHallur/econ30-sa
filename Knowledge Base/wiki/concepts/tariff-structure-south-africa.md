## Overview

South Africa's tariff schedule is applied by the **Southern African Customs Union (SACU)** common external tariff to imports from non-SACU, non-preference countries. SACU members (SA, Botswana, Lesotho, Namibia, eSwatini) share this schedule. Preferences apply under the SADC FTA, EU-SA TDCA (now EU-SADC EPA), MERCOSUR-SACU PTA, and AfCFTA.

## Headline numbers (from [[state-of-trade-policy-south-africa|Cassim et al. 2004]] and WITS / WTO updates)

- **Tariff lines**: ~6 800 (HS-8) by 2000, down from over 12 000 in the 1980s.
- **Simple average MFN tariff** 1994: ~21%; 2000: ~8%; 2023: ~7.5%.
- **Import-weighted average MFN tariff**: 1996: ~14%; 2023: ~4%.
- **Tariff peaks** (> 30%): concentrated in apparel, textiles, footwear, leather, motor vehicles, sugar, poultry.
- **Zero-rated** lines: ~50% of HS-8 by count.
- **Non-ad-valorem tariffs**: specific and mixed tariffs affect ~10% of lines (primarily agriculture and apparel); ad-valorem-equivalents computed by Cassim et al. significantly raise effective protection in those sectors.

## Effective protection

- **Nominal rate of protection (NRP)**: the tariff on final output.
- **Effective rate of protection (ERP)**: scaled by value added after tariffs on inputs — $ERP = (NRP - \sum s_i t_i)/(1 - \sum s_i)$.
- [[state-of-trade-policy-south-africa|Cassim et al.]] show ERPs in light manufacturing (clothing, auto assembly, food processing) substantially exceed their NRPs.
- Compression of the ERP schedule over 1994–2000 is one of the cleanest indicators of the liberalisation-induced protection decline.

## Special regimes

- **Motor Industry Development Programme (MIDP, 1995–2012)** and its successor **Automotive Production and Development Programme (APDP, 2013–2020)** and **APDP II (2021–)**: complex export-linked tariff rebates that effectively reduce the auto tariff for large OEMs.
- **Duty Credit Certificate Scheme (DCCS)** and **Clothing and Textile Competitiveness Programme (CTCP)**: sector support measures.
- **Rebate provisions**: Schedule 3–6 of the Customs and Excise Act permit duty-free import of listed inputs.

## Why the schedule still matters

- Even modest average tariffs mask **tariff peaks** that matter for employment-intensive sectors.
- The schedule is a **policy lever** for industrial strategy — raising or lowering duties on specific HS lines shifts effective protection in targeted sectors.
- The **ITAC** (International Trade Administration Commission) conducts tariff-change applications; outcomes shape which industries expand or contract.

## Data sources

- **ITAC** tariff schedule (current).
- **SARS** Tariff Book (applied duties; includes rebate items).
- **WTO Tariff Analysis Online** (TAO) — for MFN vs preferential comparisons.
- **WITS** (World Bank) — SACU-level tariff line data.
