## Definition

**Growth, Employment and Redistribution (GEAR)** is the macroeconomic strategy adopted by the South African Government in June 1996, replacing the [[reconstruction-and-development-programme|RDP]] as the operational macro framework for the first post-apartheid decade.

## Targets (as announced, 2000 horizon)

- **GDP growth:** 6.0% per year by 2000.
- **Employment creation:** 400 000 new formal-sector jobs per year.
- **Fiscal deficit:** ≤ 3.0% of GDP by 1999/2000 (~5% at adoption).
- **Inflation:** 7–8% by 2000 (from double digits).
- **Exports:** 8–9% real growth per year.
- **Investment:** gross fixed capital formation rising to 26% of GDP.

## Core logic

GEAR bundled five levers:

1. **Fiscal consolidation** — deficit cuts, expenditure restraint, shift from revenue borrowing to taxation-financed spending.
2. **Tight monetary policy** — explicit move toward an inflation-targeting stance (formalised by SARB in 2000).
3. **Trade liberalisation** — faster tariff reductions than the WTO schedule required, simplification of the tariff line structure.
4. **Exchange-control relaxation** — outward capital-account liberalisation for residents.
5. **Labour-market flexibility** — wage moderation, restructuring of state enterprises, limited privatisation.

The strategy assumes these reforms raise private investment, which raises labour demand, which reduces unemployment, which broadens the tax base, which finances RDP-style social spending.

## How it differs from the RDP

| Dimension | RDP (1994) | GEAR (1996) |
|---|---|---|
| Macro posture | Keynesian-developmental | Orthodox / supply-side |
| Fiscal stance | Expansionary, needs-driven | Contractionary, deficit-anchored |
| Monetary stance | Accommodative | Tight, inflation-focused |
| Trade | Gradual, conditional | Accelerated unilateral liberalisation |
| Capital account | Caution | Progressive relaxation |
| Labour market | Rights-based, union-aligned | Flexibility, wage moderation |
| Ownership | State-led | Public-private mix, partial privatisation |

## Outcomes vs targets (by ~2000)

- GDP growth averaged ~2.5% vs 6% target.
- Formal employment **declined** over 1996–2000 despite the 400 000/year target — jobs were created in later periods but never at the promised pace.
- Fiscal deficit fell below 3% of GDP — the one target met.
- Inflation fell into single digits.
- Gross fixed capital formation rose modestly but never reached 26%.
- Inequality (Gini) rose; formal manufacturing employment fell (see [[premature-deindustrialisation]] and [[mayekiso-trade-liberalisation-privatisation]]).

## Critiques in the knowledge base

- [[rdp-to-gear-transition|Adelzadeh (1996)]] — heterodox/Keynesian economic critique of GEAR's model, arithmetic, and assumptions.
- [[trade-liberalization-sa-manufacturing|Driver (2019)]] — empirical evidence that the liberalisation/exchange-rate channel compressed mark-ups and employment without robust productivity gains.
- [[trade-liberalization-local-labor-markets-south-africa|Erten-Leight-Tregenna (2018)]] — causal evidence of district-level employment losses from GEAR-era tariff cuts.
- [[political-economy-of-transition-handbook-part1|McKinley (2023)]] — political-economy reading of GEAR as the "neoliberal capture" of the ANC.

## Why it matters for the project

GEAR is the **operational pivot of post-apartheid macro policy**. Nearly every empirical question in the knowledge base — about trade, tariffs, FDI, manufacturing, labour markets, or inequality — can be framed as: *was GEAR responsible, and if so, how much?*
