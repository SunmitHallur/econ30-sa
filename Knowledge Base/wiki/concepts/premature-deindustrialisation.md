## Definition

A term coined by Dani Rodrik (2015, 2016, 2021) to describe the phenomenon of developing economies reaching peak manufacturing share of employment and output at **lower income levels** than historical industrialisers did, and subsequently losing manufacturing share faster. The "premature" qualifier signals that these economies are hitting the downslope of the industrial hump before they have converged on advanced-economy income levels or institutions.

## SA's pattern

- Manufacturing **share of GDP**: ~20% in the mid-1990s → ~13% by 2021 ([[mayekiso-trade-liberalisation-privatisation|Mayekiso & Lamla 2025]]).
- Manufacturing **share of employment**: ~17% (1996) → ~10% (2023).
- Manufacturing **jobs**: ~1.5–1.6 million in 2005 → ~1.4 million in 2023.
- **Import penetration** rose from ~17% (1990) to ~42% (2012) ([[trade-liberalization-sa-manufacturing|Driver 2019]]).

## Candidate causes

1. **Unilateral tariff liberalisation** beyond WTO requirements (GEAR era): see [[trade-liberalisation-south-africa]].
2. **Real exchange-rate volatility and episodes of appreciation** — the Dutch-disease-like mechanism documented in [[trade-liberalization-sa-manufacturing|Driver 2019]].
3. **Electricity and logistics constraints** from the mid-2000s onwards: Eskom's capacity shortfall, Transnet's rail and port congestion.
4. **Global competition**, especially from China post-2001 WTO accession (the "China shock" applied to SA's light manufacturing).
5. **Weak industrial policy**: small sectoral incentives, underfunded IDC mandate, patchy localisation.
6. **Skills mismatch**: shortages in engineering and technical skills that constrain firm scale-up.

## Measurement caveats

- Some of the apparent deindustrialisation is mechanical (services grew faster, pushing manufacturing share down).
- Real value-added in manufacturing has been flat, not negative — so the "deindustrialisation" is primarily a **relative** phenomenon in output, but an **absolute** phenomenon in employment.
- Classification conventions (ISIC v3 vs v4) introduce apparent breaks around 2008.

## Policy implications (from the knowledge base)

- [[trade-liberalization-sa-manufacturing|Driver 2019]]: the mark-up and exchange-rate evidence is more consistent with Rodrik (2008) than with Aghion et al. (2008) — support for protection or subsidy of labour-intensive manufacturing.
- [[mayekiso-trade-liberalisation-privatisation|Mayekiso & Lamla 2025]]: advocates reindustrialisation via localisation, procurement preferences, sectoral incentives, and skills alignment.
- Policy debate since 2018 centres on: automotive APDP extension, green/renewables industrial policy, EV white paper, localisation master plans (sugar, clothing, steel, chemicals, poultry).
