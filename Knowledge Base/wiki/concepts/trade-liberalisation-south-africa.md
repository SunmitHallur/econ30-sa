## Definition

**Trade liberalisation** in the SA context refers to the sustained reduction in tariff and non-tariff barriers to imports from roughly 1972 onwards, with three distinct phases post-1990: (i) GATT-driven (1990–1994); (ii) WTO / GEAR-driven (1995–2002); (iii) FTA-driven (post-2000), including the EU-SA TDCA, SADC FTA, and latterly AfCFTA.

## Timeline

- **1972–1977**: First episode, modest.
- **1983–1990**: Quantitative restrictions → tariffs; IDC reforms.
- **1990–1994**: Phasing out of import surcharges; simplification of the tariff schedule.
- **1995–2002**: WTO five-year phase-down; GEAR commits to faster-than-WTO liberalisation.
- **1999**: EU-SA Trade, Development and Cooperation Agreement (TDCA) signed.
- **2008**: SADC Free Trade Area consolidated.
- **2021**: AfCFTA implementation begins.

Source: [[state-of-trade-policy-south-africa|Cassim et al. (2004)]]; [[mayekiso-trade-liberalisation-privatisation|Mayekiso & Lamla (2025)]].

## Key stylised facts

- **Tariff lines** nearly halved from the 1980s schedule.
- **Import-weighted average MFN tariff** fell from double digits in the early 1990s to single digits by 2000 and has remained around 7–8%.
- **Trade openness** (WDI measure, see [[dataset-sa-wdi-panel]]) roughly doubled 1990–2008, from ~38% to ~66% of GDP.
- **Tariff dispersion** remains high: clothing, textiles, footwear, and some auto components carry ad-valorem-equivalent duties above 40% into the 2000s.

## Effects — the empirical record

1. **Local labour markets**: [[trade-liberalization-local-labor-markets-south-africa|Erten-Leight-Tregenna (2018)]] find that districts with larger tariff cuts suffered significant manufacturing job losses; displaced workers mostly left the labour force rather than moving to services or informal work.
2. **Manufacturing mark-ups and investment**: [[trade-liberalization-sa-manufacturing|Driver (2019)]] shows that real exchange-rate appreciation and competitive pressure compressed manufacturing mark-ups and employment without robust productivity or export gains — consistent with Rodrik's 2008 reading.
3. **Welfare and tariff structure**: [[state-of-trade-policy-south-africa|Cassim et al. (2004)]] estimate modest net welfare gains but significant sector-specific employment losses in light manufacturing.
4. **Deindustrialisation**: [[mayekiso-trade-liberalisation-privatisation|Mayekiso & Lamla (2025)]] document the 30-year trend — manufacturing share of GDP from ~19% (2005) to ~13% (2021), employment from ~1.4m to ~1.09m.

## Competing narratives

- **Mainstream / GEAR view**: Liberalisation raised efficiency, lowered input costs, and improved export competitiveness. Sectoral pain was transitional and concentrated in protected sectors.
- **Structuralist / Rodrik view**: Liberalisation compressed mark-ups, accelerated premature deindustrialisation, and in the absence of effective industrial and labour-market policies produced net welfare losses at the labour-income margin.
- **Political-economy view** ([[political-economy-of-transition-handbook-part1]]): Liberalisation was a political commitment made by the ANC-in-transition to secure external support and white-capital cooperation; its economic logic was secondary to its political function.

## Remaining open questions

- How much of manufacturing contraction is attributable to **trade** versus **electricity constraints**, **logistics failures**, and **exchange-rate volatility**?
- What was the counterfactual tariff path in the absence of GEAR — would WTO accession alone have produced a similar outcome?
- What is the aggregate welfare effect once local-labour-market spillovers are fully priced in?
