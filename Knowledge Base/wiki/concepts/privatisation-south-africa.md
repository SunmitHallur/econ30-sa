## What happened

South Africa's post-1994 programme for state-owned enterprises was more **corporatisation and partial disposal** than full-scale privatisation.

| SOE | Main event | Post-status |
|---|---|---|
| **Telkom** | 1997 strategic partnership with SBC-Telekom Malaysia; 2003 IPO | Listed; state 40% |
| **Iscor → ArcelorMittal SA** | Unbundled into Iscor/Kumba (2002); sold to Mittal (2004) | Fully privatised |
| **Sasol** | Listed 1979; post-1994 removal of residual protection | Fully private |
| **Transnet** | Corporatised 1990; rail, port, pipeline unbundling (2005–) | State-owned |
| **Eskom** | Corporatised 1987/2001; three-way unbundling announced 2019 | State-owned, in crisis |
| **SAA** | Corporatised 1998; multiple recapitalisations; partial SEP 2023 | State + partner |
| **Denel** | Corporatised 1992 | State-owned |
| **Post Office** | Corporatised 1991 | State-owned, in business rescue |
| **Water utilities** | Concessioning trials (iGoli 2002) | Mostly municipal/public |

## Heterodox reading

[[political-economy-of-transition-handbook-part1|ILRIG (2023)]] and [[mayekiso-trade-liberalisation-privatisation|Mayekiso & Lamla (2025)]] argue:

- Corporatisation imposed commercial pricing on water, electricity, rail freight, and postal services, creating affordability crises for low-income households.
- Partial privatisation shifted risks to workers (retrenchments at Telkom, Transnet, and SAA) and to consumers (tariff increases) while profits went to new private shareholders.
- The programme was often **rent-shifting** rather than efficiency-enhancing; SOE crises (Eskom, SAA) followed.

## Mainstream reading

- Privatisation was partial and halting; the real problem is **bad SOE governance**, not too much privatisation.
- Eskom and Transnet crises reflect state-capture-era procurement failures, not the post-1996 reforms.
- Full privatisation was politically impossible; what happened was the second-best corporatisation path.

## Measurable consequences

- **Electricity**: Eskom's average real tariff more than tripled 2008–2023; load-shedding costs estimated at 1–2% of GDP per year from 2022.
- **Telecommunications**: Prices fell sharply after competition (Vodacom, MTN) and the relegation of Telkom from monopoly to listed competitor.
- **Rail freight**: Transnet volumes fell from ~226 Mt (2018) to ~150 Mt (2023), reflecting operational collapse; road-freight substitution shifts costs onto users.
- **Postal services**: Real decline in volumes and reliability; business rescue filings from 2023.
