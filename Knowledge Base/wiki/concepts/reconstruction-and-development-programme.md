## Definition

The **Reconstruction and Development Programme (RDP)** is the ANC's 1994 founding policy framework — an integrated socio-economic programme intended to redress apartheid's legacy through a combination of basic-needs delivery (housing, water, electrification, health, education), economic restructuring, democratisation of state and society, and nation-building.

## Origins

- Drafted by ANC-aligned economists, unionists, and activists in 1993–1994.
- Formally adopted by the ANC, COSATU, and SACP before the April 1994 election.
- Adopted by the Government of National Unity as official policy post-election.
- Published as the **RDP White Paper** in September 1994.

## Principles

The RDP identifies six core principles:

1. An integrated and sustainable programme.
2. A people-driven process.
3. Peace and security for all.
4. Nation-building.
5. Linking reconstruction and development.
6. Democratisation of South Africa.

## Five programmes

1. **Meeting basic needs** — housing, water, electricity, land, health, education.
2. **Developing human resources** — education and training reform.
3. **Building the economy** — integrated industrial strategy, trade reform, SMME support.
4. **Democratising state and society** — reshaping local government, public service.
5. **Implementing the RDP** — financing, delivery machinery, an RDP Fund.

## Economic posture

The RDP's macroeconomic framing is broadly **Keynesian-developmental**:

- It assumes demand-led growth supported by redistribution and public investment.
- Fiscal policy is framed as a tool for developmental spending; deficits are acceptable if they finance productive investment.
- Trade reform is cautious, sector-specific, and coupled with supply-side support.
- Labour-market policy emphasises workers' rights, skills, and strong unions.

## Displacement by GEAR

- The RDP Office was **closed in 1996**; RDP functions were distributed across line ministries.
- **GEAR (1996)** replaced the RDP as the operational macro strategy, with a sharp pivot toward fiscal consolidation, tariff liberalisation, monetary tightening, and partial privatisation (see [[gear-strategy]]).
- The ILRIG handbook ([[political-economy-of-transition-handbook-part1]]) reads this displacement as the "gradual embracing of neo-liberalism" — a term adopted from [[rdp-to-gear-transition|Adelzadeh (1996)]].

## Why it matters for the project

- The RDP is the **normative benchmark** against which post-1994 economic performance is judged: basic-needs delivery, employment generation, inequality reduction, industrial diversification.
- Measuring post-1994 outcomes against the RDP's own targets rather than GEAR's targets yields systematically less favourable conclusions.
- The RDP → GEAR pivot is the pivotal institutional event in the post-1994 economic narrative.
